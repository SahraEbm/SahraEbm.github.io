From Silicon to Wide-Bandgap: The Evolution of Power and RF Semiconductor Devices

By Sahra Ebrahimmagham • Semiconductor Device Engineer & Researcher

Since the late 1950s, the silicon (Si) material system has shouldered the primary burden of the microelectronics industry for more than six decades. Silicon-based power MOSFETs have served as the workhorse for the power electronics and power-delivery markets, while Si Vertical FETs, Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJTs), Laterally Diffused MOSFETs (LDMOS), and Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBTs) have been widely deployed in high-power switching circuits — the fundamental building blocks of modern power conversion systems.

Typical structure of a Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT)

Despite continuous evolutionary improvements that have pushed silicon devices toward their performance limits, these technologies have now reached a fundamental plateau dictated by the intrinsic material constraints of silicon. At the same time, the power electronics and power-conversion industries are demanding ever-higher efficiency, power density, and cost-effectiveness.

This has driven the semiconductor community to pursue next-generation wide-bandgap (WBG) materials, with Silicon Carbide (SiC) and Gallium Nitride (GaN) emerging as the most promising pathways to overcome silicon’s limitations and deliver step-change improvements in performance.

Comparison of Si, SiC, and GaN material systems and device structures

In the RF and microwave domain, III-V compound semiconductors have long been the material of choice for high-frequency applications.
