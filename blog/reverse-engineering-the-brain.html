Reverse Engineering the Brain

By Sahra Ebrahimmagham • July 2026

Over the past few years, my research has focused on next-generation semiconductor devices—particularly gallium nitride (GaN) high-electron-mobility transistors (HEMTs) for power and RF applications. This is still the area where I feel I can make my most meaningful scientific contributions.

I truly believe GaN will remain a cornerstone technology for power conversion, high-frequency electronics, and many emerging applications for years to come.

A New Question

At the same time, I’ve found myself increasingly drawn to a very different but potentially complementary direction...

What if one of the best sources of inspiration for the future of computing isn’t another semiconductor material, but biology itself?

For decades, engineers have excelled at reverse engineering... Lately, I keep wondering if we could apply the same mindset to the most remarkable computational system we know: the human brain.

This is why I’m excited about the potential for deeper collaboration between semiconductor researchers, neuroscientists, biologists, physicists, and computer scientists...

Exploring biologically inspired computing doesn’t replace my core work on GaN HEMTs; it actually enriches it.
